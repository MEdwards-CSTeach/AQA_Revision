01

The Golden Rules

Before any technique, these apply to every question on every paper. Read them until they are automatic.

1
Identify the command term first The very first word (or short phrase) of a question tells you what to do. State, Explain, Describe, and Calculate require completely different types of answer. Underline it before you write a single word.
2
Count the marks before you write One mark almost always = one distinct point. A [4 mark] question needs four separate, creditworthy statements. If you write three sentences for a 4-marker, you will likely drop a mark. Aim for at least one more point than the mark total as insurance.
3
Use the context — always If the question gives a scenario (e.g. "a file server", "a medical diagnosis system"), your answer must relate to that context. A generic advantage of SSDs might not score if it is irrelevant to the given scenario. The examiner's report repeatedly flags this as a top reason marks are lost.
4
Make your initial statement, then clarify it State the point clearly, then develop it. "Data is immutable" by itself often scores 0. "Data is immutable, meaning its value cannot be changed once assigned, so multiple threads can read it simultaneously without causing errors" scores both marks. When in doubt, always add a clarifying sentence — it cannot lose you marks.
5
Never contradict yourself If you write something correct and then write something that contradicts it, the examiner may award zero for that point. Cross out an answer entirely if you change your mind rather than adding a conflicting sentence.
6
Re-read the question after answering With time remaining, re-read the question and check: (a) did you answer what was asked, not what you expected to be asked? (b) did you address all parts — questions with "and" in them often have two distinct mark-earning requirements.
⚠️
The "and" trap

Questions like "Describe how data is represented on a magnetic disk and how it is transmitted using TCP/IP" are two questions in one. Students who wrote only about one half scored at most 50% of the available marks regardless of answer quality. Always look for conjunctions (and, as well as, also).

02

Command Terms Decoded

Every command term has a specific meaning and demands a specific type of response. Misreading the command is the single biggest cause of unnecessary mark loss.

State
1–2 marks
Express in clear terms. No explanation required.
One precise noun/phrase is enough. Do not waste time explaining — it won't earn extra marks and wastes time you need elsewhere.
Define
1–2 marks
Specify the meaning of a term precisely.
Include what it is and, if technical, what it does. "Hardware is the physical components of a computer system" — noun + characteristic.
Describe
2–4 marks
Set out characteristics — what it is and how it works.
More than a definition. Describe the process or features. "An I/O controller acts as an interface between the CPU and peripherals, translating signals between them" — not just "it communicates."
Explain
2–6 marks
Set out purposes or reasons — the why and how.
Use "because", "so that", "which means", "therefore". An explanation that does not include a reason will not score the second mark. Make a statement then justify it.
Compare
2–4 marks
Identify similarities and/or differences.
You must reference both things. "Lossy compression reduces file size more than lossless" scores; "Lossy compression reduces file size a lot" does not. Use comparative language: more, less, whereas, unlike.
Calculate
1–4 marks
Work out a numerical value, showing your working.
Always show each step — method marks are available even for wrong final answers. Write units in your answer. State your formula before substituting numbers.
Draw
2–4 marks
Produce a diagram.
Label everything. If drawing a logic circuit, check operator precedence carefully — AND binds tighter than OR. If drawing an ERD, check relationship directions.
Discuss
4–12 marks
Present key points — balanced, with reasoning.
Extended response. Structure your answer. Cover multiple distinct points. For ethical questions, consider multiple stakeholder perspectives. Do not just list — develop each point.
Suggest
1–2 marks
Present a possible case or solution.
There may be no single correct answer — but your suggestion must be plausible and consistent with the scenario given. Justify your suggestion briefly.
❌
The Explain vs Describe mix-up

"Describe the role of an I/O controller" and "Explain the role of an I/O controller" need different things. Describe = what it does; Explain = why / how it does it. For Explain, always include a reason. "Acts as an interface" alone is a description. "Acts as an interface between the CPU and peripherals, so that signals can be translated between them, allowing them to communicate despite using different protocols" is an explanation.

03

Reading the Marks

The number of marks in brackets is a direct signal about the depth of answer required.

MarksWhat's expectedTime to spend (approx)
[1] One specific, precise point. No explanation needed. 1–2 minutes
[2] Two distinct points, OR one point fully explained/developed. 3–4 minutes
[3–4] Three or four distinct mark-worthy statements. Each needs to be separate — don't say the same thing twice in different words. 5–8 minutes
[6+] Extended answer. Plan first. Structure into clear distinct points. Quality over quantity, but ensure enough distinct points to hit the mark total. 10–18 minutes
💡
Multiple choice & "select all that apply"

Some questions ask you to select multiple true statements. Read every option fully before selecting. In June 2024, approximately 50 students selected two or more options on a question that explicitly said "select only one." These are automatic zeros. Read the instruction, not just the options.

04

Paper 1 — Programming Theory P1

Paper 1 covers 4.1 Fundamentals of Programming, 4.2 Data Structures, 4.3 Algorithms, and 4.4 Theory of Computation. It runs for 2 hours 30 minutes on-screen.

Theory questions — short answer

The theory sections on Paper 1 follow the same command term rules as Paper 2. Do not rush these because you feel the "real work" is the coding — every mark counts equally.

✅
OOP questions — use the correct terminology
  • Don't say "a function inside a class" — say method.
  • Don't say "a copy of the class" — say instance/object.
  • Don't confuse inheritance (is-a relationship) with composition (has-a). Composition uses a filled diamond in UML; aggregation uses an open diamond.
  • When explaining encapsulation, mention hiding internal state and controlling access via methods.

Trace tables

Trace table questions are among the most reliable mark-earners — the process is mechanical. Follow this approach:

1
Create a column for every variable and the output before you start tracing.
2
Execute one line at a time. Never jump ahead.
3
For loops, always check the termination condition — off-by-one errors lose marks.
4
For shift operations, draw the binary and count carefully — shifting in the wrong direction is a common error.
5
If a trace asks "what is the purpose of this program", complete the trace first — the answer usually becomes clear from the final output values.

Big-O and algorithm complexity

When asked to compare algorithms for time or space complexity: state the Big-O class for each algorithm (e.g. O(n log n) vs O(n²)), then explain what this means in terms of how performance changes as input size grows. Do not just state the notation — examiners want to know you understand its significance.

05

Skeleton Code Questions P1

The skeleton program is released in advance as Preliminary Material. Study it before the exam. Understand the overall purpose of the program, the classes/functions provided, parameter names, and return types — the exam will build on them.

1
Read the question requirement, not just the code Questions often specify exactly what to implement: "write a function that takes X and returns Y." Code that does something different, even if syntactically correct, will not score.
2
Use the existing structure If the skeleton has an existing class with methods, write your code to fit within that structure. Call existing methods/functions where appropriate — this shows understanding.
3
Test mentally with the given test data Walk through your code with the provided test data before finalising. Many errors are simple logic mistakes that a 30-second trace would catch.
4
Don't leave blank — attempt and comment Even a partial attempt with a comment explaining your approach can earn method marks. A blank answer earns nothing.
⚠️
Indentation matters in Python

In Python, a misplaced indentation is a syntax error. Ensure loop bodies, if-blocks, and function bodies are correctly indented. For Java/C#, check brace matching. The on-screen editor may not catch these for you.

Adapting/extending code

Questions may ask you to adapt existing code rather than write from scratch. Only change what is necessary — unnecessary changes to working code can break it and lose you marks. Highlight (in your head) exactly which lines need to change before you start editing.

06

Paper 2 — Written Theory P2

Paper 2 covers topics 4.5 through 4.12. It is a written exam of 2 hours 30 minutes with compulsory short and extended answer questions.

4.5 Data Representation 4.6 Computer Systems 4.7 Organisation & Architecture 4.8 Consequences of Computing 4.9 Networking 4.10 Databases 4.11 Big Data 4.12 Functional Programming

Binary / number system questions (4.5)

1
Show every step. Even if the final answer is wrong, method marks are available for correct intermediate working. Write out the column headers (128, 64, 32…) before you start.
2
Negative binary: For two's complement, check the sign bit. For floating-point, a negative mantissa means the number is negative — don't ignore it.
3
Floating point normalisation: After finding the mantissa, check that the first two bits differ (0.1xxx for positive, 1.0xxx for negative). If they don't, shift until they do and adjust the exponent accordingly.
4
Memory calculations: Use powers of 2, not powers of 10. 1 KiB = 2¹⁰, 1 GiB = 2³⁰. A 36-bit address bus gives 2³⁶ addressable locations — do not use 2¹⁶.
Floating Point — Common Error 4.5

Convert the floating-point representation to decimal. The mantissa is 10110000, exponent is 11111011 (both in two's complement).

❌ Losing marks Shifting the binary point in the wrong direction (right instead of left for a negative exponent), or ignoring the negative sign of the mantissa. Many students shift correctly but then treat a negative mantissa as positive.
✅ Correct approach Step 1: Exponent in decimal = –5 (two's complement). Step 2: Binary point shifts LEFT 5 places. Step 3: Mantissa is negative (starts with 1), so two's complement to get magnitude. Step 4: Combine sign and magnitude for final answer. Show every step.

Assembly language / fetch-execute cycle (4.7)

Trace execution step by step. Create a register table (MAR, MDR, CIR, PC, general registers) before starting. The most common errors are: shifting registers in the wrong direction, forgetting to increment the PC, and confusing the control bus with the control unit.

Assembly Trace — Process 4.7

Trace the execution of this assembly program and state its purpose.

✅ Approach Draw the table first: | Step | PC | MAR | MDR | CIR | R0 | R1 | R2 | Output |
Execute one line at a time. Complete the full trace before stating the purpose. The purpose will be evident from what the output contains. State it precisely: "The program adds an odd parity bit to a character code" is better than "it checks binary numbers."

Boolean algebra (4.6)

When simplifying, state which law or identity you are applying at each step (e.g. "By De Morgan's Law", "By X + X' = 1"). This earns method marks even if the simplification goes wrong later. Check operator precedence: NOT is applied first, then AND, then OR.

⚠️
Logic circuit operator precedence

For the expression A + B·C·D: AND is applied first, so B, C, and D are ANDed together before ORing with A. Students frequently draw an OR gate between B and C first, then AND the result with D — this is wrong. Draw brackets in your head: A + (B·C·D).

Databases (4.10)

SQL questions: INSERT INTO and UPDATE appear less frequently than SELECT and students often make errors on them. Remember: UPDATE TableName SET ... WHERE ... (not the field name after UPDATE). Enclose string/date values in quotes. For normalisation questions — do not just state "data inconsistency"; describe the problem: "data may be stored in multiple places so an update to one record may not be reflected in another, leading to inconsistent data."

Normalisation — The Vague Answer Problem 4.10

Describe two problems that might occur if a database is not fully normalised.

❌ Zero marks "Data inconsistency." / "Data redundancy." — These are labels, not descriptions. They will not score.
✅ Full marks "Data may be stored in multiple records, so if a supplier's address changes, it would need to be updated in every order record, and if one is missed the data becomes inconsistent." / "Deleting an order may result in losing the only record of a supplier's details, causing unintended deletion of information."

Networking — TCP/IP (4.9)

When answering questions about the TCP/IP stack, you must link specific tasks to specific layers. Writing "the data is packaged and sent" without naming which layer does what will not earn full marks. Know: Application (data), Transport (segments + port), Internet (packets + IP), Link (frames + MAC).

Ethical/legal questions (4.8)

These questions are often worth 6 marks or more and reward breadth and depth. A common error is focusing entirely on one issue (e.g. blame for errors) while missing others (bias in training data, data security, consent, legislation, accessibility, long-term impact on employment). Read the scenario carefully — answering about a different scenario (e.g. "replacing humans" when the question said "assisting humans") will not score.

07

Extended Response Questions Both

Questions worth 6 or more marks — especially the [12-mark] Paper 2 questions — are planned, not free-written.

1
Spend 2–3 minutes planning Jot bullet points of all the points you want to make. Cross them off as you write. This prevents repetition and ensures you don't forget a key area.
2
One mark = one idea = one paragraph For a 12-mark question, you need at least 12 distinct creditworthy statements. Keep each paragraph focused on one point.
3
Don't just list — develop every point "Data is encrypted" is one mark. "Data is encrypted so that if intercepted, it cannot be read without the decryption key, protecting user privacy" is potentially two marks — and it demonstrates understanding.
4
Cover the range the question suggests If the question has two named components (e.g. disk representation AND TCP/IP), split your answer roughly equally. Spending 90% of your answer on one half will cap your marks.

Extended response planning — quick structure

Identify all named topics in the question
Allocate marks mentally per topic
Note bullet points for each topic first
Write: point → develop → link to context
Avoid repeating the same idea differently
For ethics: think about multiple stakeholders
Re-read the question after writing, not before
Check you haven't contradicted yourself
08

Topic-Specific Traps

These are the errors that appear year after year in examiner reports. Learn to avoid them.

4.5 — Floating Point
❌ Common errorShifting binary point in the wrong direction when the exponent is negative. Treating a negative mantissa as positive.
✅ FixCheck the sign of the exponent first. Negative exponent = shift left. Always check the MSB of the mantissa for the sign.
4.6 — Flip-flops
❌ Common error"The output flips from 0 to 1 or 1 to 0 when a clock pulse arrives" — wrong. Students confuse the name "flip-flop" with the behaviour.
✅ FixAn edge-triggered D-type flip-flop acts as a memory element: on a clock pulse, the output changes to match the current value of the data input at that moment. It remembers, not toggles.
4.6 — Control Bus vs Control Unit
❌ Common errorConfusing the control bus with the control unit — they are different things. The control unit is a component of the CPU; the control bus is a communication pathway.
✅ FixControl Unit = part of CPU that decodes and coordinates instructions. Control Bus = carries control signals (read/write, clock, interrupts) between components.
4.7 — Assembly language advantages
❌ Common errorVague answers like "it is faster" or "it is more efficient."
✅ Fix"Assembly language allows a programmer to write optimised code that may execute faster or use less memory than code compiled from a high-level language, since the programmer has direct control over which registers and instructions are used."
4.7 — Imperative languages
❌ Common error"An imperative language describes what a problem is" — this is the OPPOSITE. This answer scored zero in June 2024.
✅ FixImperative = describes HOW to solve a problem step by step. Declarative = describes WHAT the problem is (e.g. SQL, Haskell). Remember: imperative = instructions/steps.
4.10 — SQL UPDATE syntax
❌ Common errorUPDATE FieldName SET ... — wrong. Putting the field name instead of the table name after UPDATE.
✅ Fix
UPDATE TableName
SET field = value
WHERE condition;
Also: remember quotes around strings and dates, and the correct delimiter format for dates.
4.12 — Functional Programming
❌ Common error"Data structures are immutable" — this alone scores 0. / "The co-domain is from natural numbers to real numbers" — this describes the function type, not the co-domain.
✅ FixAlways develop: "Data structures are immutable, meaning their values cannot be changed once assigned. This means multiple threads can read the same data simultaneously without any thread causing side effects that affect others." For co-domain: state the actual set (e.g. "the set of real numbers"), not the mapping.
4.9 — I/O Controller
❌ Common error"It is used for communication" / "It is an interface." Too vague — doesn't explain what it actually does.
✅ Fix"The I/O controller acts as an interface between the CPU and peripheral devices, translating electrical signals between them so that devices using different protocols can communicate with the processor."
09

Exam-Day Checklist Both

Before the exam

📋
  • For Paper 1: Review the Preliminary Material skeleton code. Know its structure, methods, and purpose before you sit down.
  • Confirm which programming language you are using — do not accidentally switch syntax mid-paper.
  • Revise the AQA assembly language instruction set — it is provided in the exam but knowing it saves time.

At the start of the paper

🔍
  • Skim the whole paper in the first 2–3 minutes to see what topics appear and where the large-mark questions are.
  • Identify the extended response question(s) and mentally budget more time for them.
  • Note any questions that give you a scenario or data set — you will need to refer back to it.

For every question

✅
  • Underline the command term.
  • Underline any context or named scenario.
  • Count the marks — write at least that many distinct points.
  • Make your initial statement, then clarify it. Never just name a feature; explain what it means or does.
  • If the question includes an example, reference it specifically in your answer.
  • Re-read the question after writing your answer — check you answered it, not a similar question you were expecting.

Time management

⏱️
  • Paper 1 (2h 30m): Roughly 1 mark per minute is a safe guide. Don't over-spend on early short questions.
  • Paper 2 (2h 30m): Same rule. A 12-mark question deserves ~12–15 minutes including planning.
  • Leave 5 minutes at the end to re-read anything you flagged as uncertain.
  • If stuck on a question, move on and return — a fresh perspective often helps.

The Master Tip — Say It, Prove It, Link It

For almost every question worth 2+ marks, use this structure:

Say it: Make the initial statement. ("TCP breaks data into segments.")

Prove it: Clarify or expand with specifics. ("Each segment contains the destination port number and sequence number.")

Link it: Connect to the purpose or scenario. ("This allows packets arriving out of order to be reassembled correctly at the destination.")
