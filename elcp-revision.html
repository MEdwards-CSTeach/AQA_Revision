Overview

Ethical
What SHOULD happen?
Moral principles and values — right or wrong, regardless of law
Legal
What MUST happen?
Laws and regulations — what is legally required or prohibited
Cultural
Impact on society?
How computing affects communities, ways of life and access
Privacy
Who controls data?
Right to control personal information; protection from monitoring
💡 The specification requires you to show awareness of current moral, social (ethical), legal and cultural opportunities and risks of computing, and apply ELCP thinking to real-world scenarios, case studies, and hypothetical situations.
⚖️
Ethical Issues
What SHOULD happen? — Moral principles independent of law

Key Concepts

  • Algorithmic bias – unfair outcomes baked into code
  • Informed consent – do users truly understand what they agree to?
  • Power and responsibility of developers
  • Scale: software can cause global harm or global good
  • AI decision-making – who is morally responsible?
  • Surveillance capitalism – profiting from behavioural data

Real-World Examples

  • Social media algorithms promoting harmful content
  • AI used in job recruitment filtering candidates unfairly
  • Facial recognition with racial bias
  • Self-driving cars – who is responsible in a collision?
  • News feed algorithms creating filter bubbles
  • Tech companies prioritising profit over user wellbeing
💡 Ethical ≠ Legal. Something can be legal but still unethical — e.g. selling user data legally but without full transparency.
⚖️
Legal Issues
What MUST happen? — Laws and enforceable obligations
Legislation Data Protection Act / GDPR
  • Personal data must be collected lawfully with consent
  • Data must be kept accurate, secure, and not longer than needed
  • Individuals have the right to see data held about them
  • Data must only be used for the stated purpose
  • Data breaches must be reported within 72 hours
Legislation Computer Misuse Act 1990
  • Unauthorised access to computer systems
  • Unauthorised access with intent to commit further offences
  • Unauthorised modification of computer material (e.g. malware)
  • Applies to hacking, creating/distributing viruses
Legislation Regulation of Investigatory Powers Act (RIPA)
  • Controls surveillance and interception of communications
  • Limits who can lawfully intercept data transmissions
  • Covers monitoring of internet traffic and emails by authorities
  • Balances national security against individual privacy

⚠️ Challenges for Legislators

  • Technology evolves faster than law can keep up
  • Internet is global; laws are national
  • Encryption/VPN makes criminals hard to identify
  • Legislators often lack technical expertise
  • Tech companies lobby to protect their interests
🌍
Cultural Issues
Impact on Society? — Communities, access, and social change

Key Cultural Issues

  • Digital divide – unequal access to technology
  • Technology creating / destroying jobs (automation)
  • Software embedding cultural values of its creators
  • Different cultural attitudes to privacy, copyright, surveillance
  • Social media changing communication norms
  • Over-reliance on technology / deskilling

Opportunities & Risks

  • Global communication and collaboration made possible
  • Open-source culture democratising software
  • E-learning expanding access to education worldwide
  • Fake news / misinformation spreading at scale
  • Cultural homogenisation – dominance of Western tech firms
  • Social isolation despite digital 'connection'
💡 Remember: software and algorithms embed moral and cultural values. A developer's choices reflect their culture and priorities.
🔒
Privacy Issues
Who Controls Data? — Personal information and monitoring

Data Collection & Consent

  • Who collects data and for what purpose?
  • Was informed consent genuinely given?
  • Are users aware of what is being collected?
  • Can people opt out without losing access to a service?

Data Storage & Security

  • Where is data stored (country matters for law)?
  • Who has access to the stored data?
  • How long is data retained?
  • What happens if data is breached?

Surveillance & Monitoring

  • CCTV, facial recognition in public spaces
  • Employer monitoring of staff communications
  • Government surveillance of internet traffic
  • Behavioural tracking by advertisers

Data Sharing & Ownership

  • Who owns data – the person or the company?
  • Can companies share or sell personal data?
  • Right to be forgotten
  • International data transfer rules (GDPR restrictions)

Exam Strategy

E
Read the scenario first; underline the technology involved. Ask: 'What values are at stake? Who could be harmed?'
L
Identify relevant legislation by name (DPA/GDPR, CMA, RIPA). Name the law + explain which provision applies to this scenario.
C
Think about groups of people: workers, communities, countries. Who benefits? Who is disadvantaged? What changes for society?
P
Follow the data: who collects it, why, where it is stored, who accesses it, and whether consent was properly obtained.
📋 General rules: Always tie your point back to the scenario context. Use ELCP as a checklist for extended questions. Name laws for legal points. For 'discuss' questions, show BOTH sides.

Question Types & How to Answer

State / Name
[1–2 marks]

Give a fact. No explanation needed. E.g. 'Name a relevant piece of legislation' → 'Data Protection Act / GDPR'.

Explain / Describe
[2–4 marks]

Make a point THEN develop it with a reason or consequence tied to the scenario. One mark per explained point.

Discuss
[4–6 marks, levels]

Cover MULTIPLE perspectives. Use ELCP as headings. Show both benefits AND risks. Link each point to the scenario. Aim for Level 3: coherent argument with range of developed points.

Evaluate / Analyse
[4–6 marks, levels]

Like 'discuss' but reach a conclusion or judgement. Weigh up the evidence. Show you understand the complexity of the issue.

⚠️ For level-marked questions: don't just list points – DEVELOP each one. 'Data may be stolen' → 'If data is stolen, users' financial details could be accessed by criminals, causing financial harm and loss of trust in the organisation.'

Worked Example – Applying ELCP to a Scenario

Scenario: A supermarket introduces a loyalty card scheme. The system collects data on every purchase and uses algorithms to send personalised offers. Data is shared with third-party marketing companies.
Ethical

Is it ethical to profile customers' purchasing habits without their full understanding? Should offers be targeted at vulnerable customers (e.g. promoting alcohol)? Algorithms may reveal sensitive information (e.g. inferring pregnancy from buying patterns).

Legal

GDPR requires consent to collect personal data and limits how it is shared with third parties. Data must be kept securely and only used for the stated purpose. Customers have the right to access and delete their data.

Cultural

Changes shopping culture – customers may feel pressured to participate to receive discounts. May disadvantage those without smartphones or internet access (digital divide). Could alter how society views commercial use of personal data.

Privacy

Who owns the purchase data – the customer or the supermarket? Is consent genuinely informed? Sharing with third parties raises risk of data breach and loss of privacy. Where is data stored (jurisdiction matters for legal protection)?

Before You Answer – Student Checklist

✓ ETHICAL
Have I identified WHO could be harmed or who benefits?
Have I considered whether this is right/fair, regardless of legality?
Have I thought about the developer's responsibility and scale of impact?
✓ LEGAL
Have I named a specific law? (DPA/GDPR, CMA, RIPA)
Have I explained which provision of the law applies to this scenario?
Have I mentioned challenges of enforcing the law (if relevant)?
✓ CULTURAL
Have I considered the impact on groups/communities/society?
Have I thought about the digital divide, job displacement, or social change?
Have I considered cultural differences in attitudes to this issue?
✓ PRIVACY
Have I traced: who collects data → why → how stored → who accesses it?
Was consent properly obtained? Is the use proportionate?
Have I considered data breaches, data sharing, and individuals' rights?
